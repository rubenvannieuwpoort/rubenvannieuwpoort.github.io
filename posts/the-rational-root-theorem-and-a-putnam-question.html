The rational root theorem and a Putnam question

I came across the following Putnam question yesterday:

B1 Let P(x)=cnxn+cn−1x.n−1..+c0P(x) = c_n x^n + c_{n - 1} x^{n - 1} _ ... + c_0P(x)=cn​xn+cn−1​x.n−1​..+c0​ be a polynomial with integer coefficients. Suppose that rrr is a rational number such that P(r)=0P(r) = 0P(r)=0. Show that the nnn numbers

are integers.

If you’re familiar with the rational root theorem you will probably see a connection. Indeed, the proof that the question asks for is along the same lines.

I have to admit I didn’t remember the proof of the rational root theorem. It’s one of those proofs that’s not very hard once you know the trick, but if you don’t know the trick, you can spend quite some time without finding the proof. It took me a while to find it again, so I will document it here for myself.

Definition: A fraction pq\frac{p}{q}qp​ is called reduced if p∈Zp \in \mathbb{Z}p∈Z, q∈Nq \in \mathbb{N}q∈N, and gcd⁡(p,q)=1\gcd(p, q) = 1gcd(p,q)=1.

Theorem (rational root theorem): Suppose that fff is a polynomial with integer coefficients c0,c1,...,cn∈Zc_0, c_1, ..., c_n \in \mathbb{Z}c0​,c1​,...,cn​∈Z:
f(x)=c0+c1x+...cnxnf(x) = c_0 + c_1 x + ... c_n x^n f(x)=c0​+c1​x+...cn​xn

If the reduced fraction pq\frac{p}{q}qp​ is a root of fff, then p∣c0p | c_0p∣c0​ and q∣cnq | c_nq∣cn​.

Proof: Since pq\frac{p}{q}qp​ is a root of fff, we have
c0+c1pq+...+cn(pq)n=0c_0 + c_1 \frac{p}{q} + ... + c_n (\frac{p}{q})^n = 0c0​+c1​qp​+...+cn​(qp​)n=0

After multiplying by qnq^nqn we obtain

c0qn+c1qn−1p+...+cnpn=0c_0 q^n + c_1 q^{n - 1}p + ... + c_n p^n = 0c0​qn+c1​qn−1p+...+cn​pn=0

Now subtract c0qnc_0 q^nc0​qn and factor out the factor ppp on the left side:

p(c1qn−1+c2qn−2p+...cnpn−1)=−c0qnp(c_1q^{n - 1} + c_2 q^{n - 2} p + ... c_n p^{n - 1}) = -c_0 q^np(c1​qn−1+c2​qn−2p+...cn​pn−1)=−c0​qn

Since the left-hand side is a product of two integers, ppp is a divisor of the left-hand side, so it is also a divisor of the right hand side. So we have p∣c0qnp | c_0 q^np∣c0​qn. Since pq\frac{p}{q}qp​ is a reduced fraction, we have gcd⁡(p,q)=1\gcd(p, q) = 1gcd(p,q)=1. It follows that p∣c0p | c_0p∣c0​. Switching the roles of ppp and qqq, and c0c_0c0​ and cnc_ncn​, we can deduce that q∣cnq | c_nq∣cn​ in an analogous way. □\square□

Note: If the polynomial fff has rational coefficients, it can easily transformed to a polynomial with the same roots and integer coefficients by multiplying it by the least common multiple of all the denominators.

The proof that the Putnam question asks for can be derived in a similar way. In the proof we will use the following lemma:

Lemma: If ab\frac{a}{b}ba​ and cd\frac{c}{d}dc​ are fractions with ab=cd\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}ba​=dc​ and gcd⁡(b,d)=1\gcd(b,d) = 1gcd(b,d)=1, then ab\frac{a}{b}ba​ and cd\frac{c}{d}dc​ are integers.

Proof: Let pq\frac{p}{q}qp​ be the reduced form of ab\frac{a}{b}ba​, so that we have pq=ab=cd\frac{p}{q} = \frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}qp​=ba​=dc​. Likewise, there exists an n∈Nn \in \mathbb{N}n∈N such that c=npc = npc=np and d=nqd = nqd=nq. Now we have gcd⁡(b,d)=gcd⁡(mq,nq)=1\gcd(b, d) = \gcd(mq, nq) = 1gcd(b,d)=gcd(mq,nq)=1. Since q∣gcd⁡(mq,nq)q | \gcd(mq, nq)q∣gcd(mq,nq), it follows that q=1q = 1q=1. So we have ab=cd=pq=p\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d} = \frac{p}{q} = pba​=dc​=qp​=p. □\square□
